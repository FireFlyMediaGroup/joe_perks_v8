## Operating principles (read before first outreach)

These shape every message and call. They're from *The Mom Test* (Rob Fitzpatrick) plus marketplace-specific guardrails.

1. **Talk about their life, not our idea.** The pre-mortem's E-3 elephant ("built from PRD, not discovery") means we haven't validated *their* problem yet. First five conversations are discovery, not sales.
2. **Past behavior, not future intent.** "What have you tried?" beats "Would you use this?"
3. **Ask for a commitment at the end of every call.** A pilot commitment is time, reputation, or money — anything that costs the prospect something. "Let's keep in touch" is a fail signal.
4. **Concierge > self-serve for pilot.** Do the manual work for them. The goal is learning and a signed pilot, not onboarding efficiency.
5. **Separate discovery from pitch.** Don't blend the two in one call. Pattern: discovery call → proposal → pilot agreement → onboarding.

## Pilot targets

### Roasters (N=3)
Ideal profile:
- Independent specialty roaster, 1–10 employees, roasting 500–5,000 lb/month.
- Already sells online (Shopify, Squarespace, or similar) with fulfillment muscle.
- Located in a city with an active specialty-coffee scene (lower CAC for brand trust).
- Owner-operator who can sign an agreement in a day, not a week.

Disqualifiers:
- Roasts wholesale-only; no DTC muscle.
- Already in a major fundraising platform partnership.
- Owner is impossible to reach directly.

### Orgs (N=3)
Ideal profile:
- PTA / PTO, sports booster club, or small nonprofit that has run a fundraiser in the last 12 months (cookie dough, wrapping paper, popcorn, etc.).
- Community size: 50–500 active supporters. Small enough for a concierge rollout; large enough to produce enough orders to stress-test the money path.
- A specific named champion (parent, coach, treasurer) who owns the fundraiser.

Disqualifiers:
- Purely digital org with no physical community (harder to mobilize).
- Running a fundraiser next week (too late).
- Have never fundraised (no benchmark, harder to sell ROI).

## Part 1 — Roaster outreach

### 1A. Cold email (first touch)

**Subject lines (pick one; A/B if sending at volume)**
- `Quick question about fundraising orders`
- `Idea for <Roaster Name> — coffee fundraisers that actually work`
- `Coffee + school fundraisers — 15 min chat?`

**Body**

> Hi <First Name>,
>
> I'm Chris, founder of Joe Perks. I'm building a platform that lets specialty roasters like <Roaster Name> run coffee fundraisers for schools and sports teams — the kind of thing that's usually done with cookie dough or wrapping paper.
>
> I'm not pitching anything yet. I'm trying to learn from three roasters what's actually hard about selling to groups like this, and what would make it worth your time. I'd love 15 minutes to ask a few questions — especially about how you currently handle one-off bulk orders, church groups, or team orders if any reach you.
>
> I can work around whatever schedule makes sense. If fundraisers aren't relevant to your business, no worries at all — a two-line reply saying so is already more than I'd expect.
>
> — Chris
> <email> · <phone>
> joeperks.com (site coming soon)

**Rules**:
- Personalize the first sentence after "Hi" with something specific from their site, a podcast they've been on, or a product you actually drink. If you can't, don't send it.
- Max one follow-up after 5 business days. Two follow-ups max. Then leave them alone for 60 days.

### 1B. Warm intro (preferred path)

Use LinkedIn or mutual contacts first. Ask the mutual connection:

> Hi <Connector>, do you happen to know <Roaster Owner> at <Roaster Name>? I'm building a coffee-fundraising platform and I'd love 15 minutes of their time — I don't need a sales intro, just a "Chris is worth talking to" two-liner if you're comfortable. Totally fine if not.

Warm intros convert 3–5x cold email. Exhaust your warm network before the cold list.

### 1C. 30-minute discovery call script (roaster)

**Opening (2 min)**

> Thanks for the time. Quick context: I'm not here to pitch anything today — I'm still learning. I'm going to ask about your business, and then near the end I'll tell you what I'm building and see if it's relevant. Is that OK?

**Current state (8 min)** — *listen, don't interrupt*

1. Walk me through a typical week at <Roaster Name>. Where does your coffee actually go?
2. Have you ever had a school or a sports team reach out asking for fundraising coffee? What happened?
3. If so, how did you price it? How did it feel — easy, painful, not worth it?
4. Where does most of your DTC volume come from today? Your own site, Amazon, wholesale, a subscription service?
5. What's the largest single order you've shipped in the last 6 months, and what was the context?

**Pains & workarounds (8 min)**

6. What are the top one or two things that eat your time that aren't actually making coffee?
7. How do you feel about Shopify apps in general — are there ones you actively love or hate?
8. When someone asks you to do something custom (fundraiser, co-branded, gift boxes), what's your gut reaction: interested, annoyed, depends?
9. When you've said yes to something custom, what's the part you wish someone else handled?

**Softly introduce Joe Perks (5 min)** — *now you can pitch*

> Here's what I'm building, at a high level. <30-second description>. The idea is: the org handles marketing, you handle roasting and shipping, we handle the plumbing and split the money three ways. Platform takes 5% plus $1 minimum per order; the org takes whatever % they choose between 5 and 25; you keep the rest after Stripe fees and shipping is passed through at cost.
>
> I'd love your reaction. What feels off? What would you push back on?

**Ask for commitment (5 min)** — *this is the whole point of the call*

Pick one of these based on how it went:

- *Strong signal*: "I'm looking for three roasters to run a pilot starting in <month>. Would you be open to committing to a pilot — I'd give you priority onboarding, a direct line to me, and we'd cap at 200 orders for the first campaign so it's manageable. Can I send over a one-page agreement this week?"
- *Interested but not ready*: "Totally understand. What would need to be true for you to say yes to a pilot? If I send a 1-page memo that addresses those, can we talk again in two weeks?"
- *Not a fit*: "Got it — it sounds like this isn't for you right now. Mind if I keep you in the loop when we launch? And if anyone in your network comes to mind who would be excited about this, I'd be grateful for the intro."

**Close (2 min)**

> Regardless of what you decide, thanks for the honesty. I'll send a quick recap email. If you think of anything after, email me — I read everything.

### 1D. Pilot agreement (one-pager)

Send within 24 hours of a yes or soft-yes. Structure:

```
JOE PERKS — ROASTER PILOT AGREEMENT (v1 launch cohort)

Parties: Joe Perks <entity TBD> and <Roaster LLC>

Term: From pilot launch through <go-live + 90 days>. Either side may exit with 14 days notice.

Economics:
- Platform fee: 5% of product subtotal, $1.00 minimum per order
- Org fee: 5–25% of product subtotal, set per campaign by the org
- Shipping: 100% passthrough to Roaster at cost
- Stripe fees: 2.9% + $0.30 on gross (charged to Roaster)
- Payout hold: 30 days post-order confirmation (standard marketplace protection)

Roaster commits to:
- Ship every order within 48 hours of purchase (SLA)
- Respond to support escalations within 24 hours during business days
- Provide product, pricing, shipping rules, and images at onboarding
- Not contract with competing coffee-fundraising platforms during the pilot

Joe Perks commits to:
- Onboard Roaster within 7 days of countersignature
- Provide a dedicated support contact (Chris) for the pilot
- Cover any chargeback costs up to $500 total during the pilot (as a risk backstop)
- Monthly 30-minute check-in call

Data: Roaster retains ownership of customer and order data; Joe Perks acts as processor.

[counsel-reviewed signature block]
```

**Note**: This is a **pilot** agreement, deliberately lighter than final roaster ToS. The full ToS (LB-4 in pre-mortem) is separate.

## Part 2 — Org outreach

### 2A. Cold email (first touch)

**Subject lines**
- `Question about your last school fundraiser`
- `Idea for <Org Name> — coffee instead of cookie dough`
- `<Town> coffee fundraiser — 15 min?`

**Body**

> Hi <First Name>,
>
> I'm Chris, building Joe Perks — a way for groups like <Org Name> to fundraise by selling local specialty coffee instead of cookie dough or wrapping paper.
>
> I'm not selling anything yet. I'm trying to learn from three organizations — a PTA, a booster club, and a nonprofit — what's actually hard about running a fundraiser and what would make one worth doing again. I'd love 15 minutes of your time.
>
> If you're the right person, I'd be grateful for the conversation. If not, no problem — even a two-line reply telling me who to reach out to instead would be a huge help.
>
> — Chris
> <email> · <phone>

### 2B. Warm intro (preferred path)

Same principle as roaster. Parents talk. A single yes from a school PTA often opens two or three more.

### 2C. 30-minute discovery call script (org)

**Opening (2 min)**: Same pattern as roaster.

**Current state (10 min)**

1. How many fundraisers does <Org Name> run per year?
2. What's the last fundraiser you ran, and how did it go — what worked, what didn't?
3. How much money do you typically raise per campaign? How much of that is profit vs. overhead?
4. How do you get the word out? Email list, social, word of mouth?
5. What's the biggest hassle — collecting money, delivering product, sign-ups, getting volunteers?
6. Who is the actual buyer — parents, community members, grandparents out of state?

**Pains (8 min)**

7. If you could wave a wand and change one thing about running a fundraiser, what would it be?
8. Have you ever tried something online (Shopify, GoFundMe, a platform) for a fundraiser? What happened?
9. What does your org's leadership need to see in a fundraiser before they'll green-light it — a specific dollar amount, a guarantee, a certain product type?

**Softly introduce Joe Perks (5 min)**

> Here's the idea. <30-second description>. Your org gets its own branded storefront featuring a specialty coffee roaster. Supporters buy directly; roaster ships directly; your org gets 5–25% of the product sale automatically deposited. No inventory, no collecting checks, no packing bags. You decide the % you want; we handle everything else.

**Ask for commitment (5 min)**

- *Strong signal*: "I'm looking for three organizations to run launch campaigns in <month>. Would you be open to running your next fundraiser with us? I'd pair you with a roaster you'd love, do the campaign setup with you personally, and cap the first run at 200 orders so it's manageable. Can I send a 1-page pilot outline?"
- *Interested but later*: "When's your next fundraiser? Would you pencil us in now, and we can confirm two weeks before?"
- *Not a fit*: Same as roaster.

**Close**: Same pattern.

### 2D. Pilot agreement (one-pager)

```
JOE PERKS — ORG PILOT AGREEMENT (v1 launch cohort)

Parties: Joe Perks <entity TBD> and <Org Name>

Term: Single campaign, up to 200 orders or 45 days, whichever comes first.

Economics:
- Org receives: <org %, chosen by org, 5–25%> of product subtotal on every order
- No upfront fee, no inventory risk, no handling of payments

Org commits to:
- Promote the campaign to its community (email, social, in-person events as appropriate)
- Appoint one campaign lead (name + email + phone) for the pilot
- Allow Joe Perks to use the campaign's results (with approval) as a case study

Joe Perks commits to:
- Set up the branded storefront with the org's logo, colors, and messaging
- Pair the org with a vetted roaster appropriate to its community
- Provide promotional templates (email copy, social posts, printable flyers)
- Answer support questions from buyers within 24 hours during business days
- Send the org a transparent, line-itemized payout report at campaign close

[signature block]
```

## Part 3 — Shared mechanics

### 3A. Outreach tracker

Keep a simple spreadsheet or Notion table — *not* a CRM yet. Columns:

| Prospect | Type (roaster/org) | Source | First contact | Last action | Status | Next step | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Status values: `not_contacted`, `contacted`, `call_scheduled`, `discovery_done`, `proposal_sent`, `pilot_signed`, `declined`, `later`.

**Weekly review**: Every Monday. Any prospect with `status = call_scheduled` or `proposal_sent` for > 7 days gets an explicit nudge or a decision to close out.

### 3B. Call prep checklist (do this before every call)

- [ ] Read their website, last 3 Instagram posts, and LinkedIn.
- [ ] Know one specific thing that makes them interesting (a podcast, a product, a news mention).
- [ ] Have the discovery-call script open but not visible to them.
- [ ] Calendar set to 30 min with a 15-min buffer after.
- [ ] Note-taking surface open. Name, date, notes, direct quotes, ask-for-commitment moment.

### 3C. Call debrief template (fill within 1 hour after every call)

These debriefs are the single most valuable artifact from this phase. File them in `docs/discovery/2026-calls/<YYYY-MM-DD>-<name>.md`. A folder of 20 of these is the honest answer to Elephant E-3.

### 3D. When to stop

If after **20 discovery calls** you cannot sign 3 roasters and 3 orgs at the pilot terms, the pre-mortem's phased-alternative #1 (roaster-only private beta) becomes the right move. Don't white-knuckle through. The data is telling you something — either the offer, the audience, or the timing is wrong. Re-run a smaller pre-mortem on that question.

### 3E. Honest inputs that you should ignore

Some things people say that mean nothing:
- "This is a great idea" → compliments are politeness, not data.
- "I'd use that" → hypothetical; means nothing until money or time changes hands.
- "You should talk to X" → useful only if they make the intro themselves.

Some things that **are** signal:
- They immediately schedule a follow-up without being asked.
- They describe a specific past event in detail.
- They introduce you to someone else unprompted.
- They ask about pricing before you bring it up.
- They negotiate the pilot terms (means they're thinking about saying yes).
